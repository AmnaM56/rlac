Did you know that honey mushrooms are the world’s largest living organism? 

This strange opening question might leave you wondering why an essay about text analysis begins with a fun(-gi) fact. However, that brief moment of confusion illustrates the central argument of this response: information rarely speaks for itself. Just like in the opening, visualizations generated through computational text analysis require context in order to be properly interpreted. In this response, I test this hypothesis with several visualizations produced from my corpus using Voyant Tools and R, and test how a large language model (LLM), Grok, interprets them when no context is provided.

## 2. Background Corpus Tools and Visualizations

Our corpus contains five texts: two canonical *Harry Potter* novels and three *Harry Potter* fanfiction texts. The two J.K. Rowling novels were *Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire* and *Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows*. The three fanfiction texts were *The Best of Fathers* by Guitérres De La Torre, *Harry Potter: Djinn Awakened* by Invaderdoom/Wishmaster1983, and *Dragonheart Caravan* by Witchdragon.

Our research question was: how does the use of explicit violent language differ between Rowling’s novels and *Harry Potter* fanfiction, and what does this suggest about how each text portrays conflict? Using two computational analysis tools, Voyant Tools and R (the latter of which used a violence dictionary of 124 terms), we produced several visuals, examples shown in Figures 1–4.

## 3. LLM Interpretation Without Context Voyant Contexts

I uploaded Figure 1 to Grok with the prompt *“Interpret this visualization”* without providing any explanation of the corpus or the methods used to generate the visualization. Figure 5 shows that Grok was able to recognize certain structural elements of the visualization, such as identifying different grammatical and contextual forms of the word *kill* within what it correctly recognized as a concordance-style display. In this sense, the figure speaks for itself to a limited extent, particularly in revealing fine-grained textual patterns.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok1.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 5. Grok’s response to the prompt “Interpret this visualization” with Figure 1 attached.*

However, without contextual information, Grok filled in gaps with incorrect assumptions about the source text and the broader purpose of the visualization. As shown in Figure 5, this misunderstanding appears to stem from extrapolation from the fanfiction author’s surname, Gutiérrez. Grok suggested that the visualization likely came from a single *Harry Potter* fanfiction text authored by someone named Gutiérrez. While it is true that this author appears in the corpus, the model failed to recognize that the concordance visualization was generated from a corpus of five different texts.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok2.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 6. Continued portion of Grok’s response to the prompt “Interpret this visualization.”*

Figure 6 further illustrates how the absence of context led Grok to construct a misleading interpretation. It assumed that the visualization reflected a single “dark, threat-heavy, Dumbledore-assassination-focused fanfiction” centered heavily around killing Dumbledore. This interpretation distorts the purpose of the analysis, which was designed to compare patterns of violent language across multiple canonical and fanfiction texts rather than to analyze a single narrative. This demonstrates a broader challenge in visual communication: even when patterns appear visible, the meaning of those patterns depends on the context in which the visualization was produced.

## 4. LLM Interpretation With Context Follow-up Prompt

To test whether additional context would improve the interpretation, I followed up by prompting Grok again, this time explaining the corpus, research question, and purpose of the visualization:

*“This visualization is a concordance view from Voyant Tools showing instances of the word ‘kill’ across a corpus of five Harry Potter texts (two canonical novels and three fanfiction works). The goal is to compare how violent language is used across these texts. How would you interpret this visualization now?”*

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok3.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 7. Grok’s response to the follow-up prompt.*

Evidently, Figure 7 shows a significantly more nuanced interpretation once contextual information was provided. With knowledge of the corpus and research objective, Grok correctly recognized that the concordance highlights differences in how violent language—specifically the word *kill*—appears across canonical *Harry Potter* texts and fanfiction. The model suggested that fanfiction may intensify certain violent motifs through repetition, which can make fan works appear lexically more violent than the original novels. It also observed that Rowling’s novels often use the word *kill* sparingly during major narrative moments, while fanfiction may explore the motif more repeatedly through emotional or narrative elaboration. Although some interpretations remained speculative, the response demonstrates that providing contextual information allows the LLM to move beyond guesswork and produce a more meaningful reading of the visualization.

## 5. LLM Interpretation With More Visual Context No Follow-up Prompts

To further explore how context affects interpretation, I uploaded the heatmap produced in R (Figure 4) to Grok without providing additional explanation. Unlike the Voyant concordance visualization, the heatmap contains labeled axes and clearly separated texts, which provide more structural context for the viewer.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok4.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 8. Grok’s response to the heatmap visualization when prompted only with “Interpret this visualization.”*

As shown in Figure 8, Grok correctly identified that the rows represent violence-related vocabulary while the columns correspond to different *Harry Potter* texts. It also interpreted the color gradient as representing differences in word frequency. Building on this structural understanding, Grok observed that *Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows* displayed the highest concentration of words such as *death* and *kill*, suggesting that it is the most violence-word-dense text in the dataset. This interpretation demonstrates that the heatmap’s visual structure communicates certain contextual clues even without an explanatory prompt. However, the model still lacked knowledge of the methodological decisions behind the visualization, such as the predefined violence dictionary and normalization per 10,000 words. Even well-structured visualizations therefore benefit from accompanying explanation.

## 6. Context and Visual Interpretation in Scholarship

The importance of context in interpreting visualizations is also emphasized in digital humanities scholarship and discussions of distant reading. In the *Distant Reading* conversation with Ama Bemma Adwetewa-Badu on the *New Books Network*, distant reading is described as a method that allows scholars to identify patterns across large collections of texts that would otherwise be impossible to read individually. However, the discussion also stresses that these patterns only become meaningful when they are interpreted within a broader literary and historical context. Similarly, research on misleading visualizations demonstrates that charts can easily be misunderstood when viewers lack sufficient explanatory information. A study from the Visualization Design Lab explains that misleading charts often emerge not because they are intentionally deceptive, but because viewers fill in gaps between what they see and what they assume the data represents (Lisnic et al.). This observation closely parallels Grok’s behavior in this experiment: when contextual information was missing, the model attempted to construct its own narrative about the visualization, which led to incorrect conclusions. This dynamic also reflects what Mike Kestemont and Luc Herman describe in *Can Machines Read (Literature)?*, where computational methods can reveal patterns across large textual corpora but still require human interpretation to connect those patterns to meaningful literary insights. Together, these perspectives reinforce the central argument of this essay that visualizations require contextual framing in order to communicate meaning accurately.

## 7. Conclusion

This experiment demonstrates that visualizations rarely speak entirely on their own. When Grok interpreted the Voyant concordance without context, it recognized some structural features but ultimately filled in missing information with incorrect assumptions. Providing additional context through a follow-up prompt significantly improved the interpretation, while the heatmap visualization showed that some figures contain more built-in contextual clues through labeled axes and structured comparisons. If a reader encountered these visuals in a fellow student’s paper, they would likely rely on surrounding explanations to understand the corpus, the violence dictionary, and the normalization methods that shaped the data. In other words, successful visual communication requires both visual literacy and contextual framing from the author. The additional visualizations produced in this project, such as the individual and comparative word clouds (Figures 2 and 3), illustrate alternative ways of representing the corpus, but they too depend on explanation to clarify what patterns they are meant to highlight. Testing visualizations with an LLM therefore becomes a useful strategy for improving academic writing: if a model misinterprets a figure, it signals that more context may be needed. Just as the opening fungi fact initially appeared random until its context was explained, data visualizations—like mushrooms—reveal their meaning only when we understand the environment in which they grow, a reminder echoed in both distant-reading scholarship and research on misleading visualizations.
