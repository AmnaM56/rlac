Did you know that honey mushrooms are the world’s largest living organism?

At first, this question may seem unrelated to computational text analysis. Yet that moment of confusion illustrates the central argument of this response: information rarely speaks for itself. Visualizations generated through computational text analysis require context in order to be interpreted meaningfully. In this response, I test this idea using several visualizations produced from my corpus with Voyant Tools and R, examining how a large language model (LLM), Grok, interprets them when no contextual explanation is provided.

## 2. Background Corpus Tools and Visualizations

Our corpus consists of five texts: two canonical *Harry Potter* novels and three *Harry Potter* fanfiction works. The Rowling novels are *Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire* and *Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows*. The fanfiction texts include *The Best of Fathers* by Guitérres De La Torre, *Harry Potter: Djinn Awakened* by Invaderdoom/Wishmaster1983, and *Dragonheart Caravan* by Witchdragon.

The research question guiding this analysis asks how the use of explicit violent language differs between Rowling’s novels and *Harry Potter* fanfiction, and what this reveals about how conflict is portrayed. Using Voyant Tools and R—where a violence dictionary of 124 terms was applied—we produced several visualizations (Figures 1–4) that illustrate patterns of violent vocabulary across the corpus.

## 3. LLM Interpretation Without Context Voyant Contexts

I uploaded Figure 1 to Grok with the prompt *“Interpret this visualization”* without explaining the corpus or analytical methods. As shown in Figure 5, the model correctly recognized the visualization as a concordance-style display and identified patterns in how the word *kill* appears across sentences. In this sense, the visualization communicated some structural information on its own.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok1.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 5. Grok’s response to the prompt “Interpret this visualization” with Figure 1 attached.*

However, Grok quickly filled missing context with incorrect assumptions. It inferred that the visualization represented a single *Harry Potter* fanfiction text authored by someone named Gutiérrez, likely based on the surname appearing in the corpus. In reality, the concordance was generated from five texts.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok2.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 6. Continued portion of Grok’s response to the prompt “Interpret this visualization.”*

Figure 6 further shows how the absence of context led Grok to construct a misleading interpretation. It assumed that the visualization reflected a “dark” fanfiction centered on killing Dumbledore. These conclusions demonstrate how visible patterns can still be misinterpreted when viewers lack the contextual information needed to understand how a visualization was produced.

## 4. LLM Interpretation With Context Follow-up Prompt

To test whether additional context would improve interpretation, I prompted Grok again while explaining the corpus and research goal:

*“This visualization is a concordance view from Voyant Tools showing instances of the word ‘kill’ across a corpus of five Harry Potter texts (two canonical novels and three fanfiction works). The goal is to compare how violent language is used across these texts.”*

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok3.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 7. Grok’s response to the follow-up prompt.*

With this context, Grok produced a significantly more accurate interpretation. It recognized that the concordance compares how the word *kill* appears across canonical and fanfiction texts. The model suggested that fanfiction may emphasize violent motifs more repeatedly, while Rowling’s novels tend to use the word more sparingly during key narrative moments. Although some aspects of the interpretation remained speculative, the response demonstrates how contextual framing improves the meaningful interpretation of visualizations.

## 5. LLM Interpretation With More Visual Context No Follow-up Prompts

To explore whether visual structure alone could provide context, I uploaded the heatmap produced in R (Figure 4) to Grok using the same prompt.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/Grok4.png' | relative_url }}">

*Figure 8. Grok’s response to the heatmap visualization when prompted only with “Interpret this visualization.”*

Unlike the concordance visualization, the heatmap includes labeled axes and separated texts. Grok correctly identified that rows represented violence-related vocabulary and columns represented different *Harry Potter* texts. It also recognized that darker colors indicated higher frequencies, noting that *Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows* contained the highest concentration of terms such as *death* and *kill*. While the model interpreted the visual structure successfully, it still lacked knowledge of methodological details such as the violence dictionary and normalization per 10,000 words. Even structured visualizations therefore benefit from explanation.

## 6. Context and Visual Interpretation in Scholarship

The importance of context in interpreting visualizations is also emphasized in digital humanities scholarship. In the *Distant Reading* discussion with Ama Bemma Adwetewa-Badu on the *New Books Network*, distant reading is described as a method that identifies patterns across large textual collections. However, those patterns only become meaningful when interpreted within broader literary contexts. Research on misleading visualizations similarly shows that charts can be misunderstood when viewers lack explanatory information. The Visualization Design Lab notes that misleading charts often arise not from intentional deception but from viewers filling gaps between visual patterns and assumptions about the data (Lisnic et al.). This dynamic resembles Grok’s behavior in this experiment, where the model constructed its own narrative when contextual information was absent. As Kestemont and Herman argue, computational methods reveal patterns but still require human interpretation to produce meaningful literary insight.

## 7. Conclusion

This experiment demonstrates that visualizations rarely communicate meaning entirely on their own. When Grok interpreted the Voyant concordance without context, it identified structural features but produced incorrect assumptions about the data. Providing additional explanation significantly improved the interpretation, while the heatmap showed that some visualizations contain partial contextual clues through labeled axes and structured comparisons.

If readers encountered these visuals in another student’s paper, they would likely rely on surrounding explanation to understand the corpus, methodology, and research goals. Successful visual communication therefore requires both visual literacy and contextual framing from the author. Testing visualizations with an LLM can also help improve academic writing: if a model misinterprets a figure, it may signal that additional explanation is needed. Just as the opening fungi fact initially seemed random until its context was explained, visualizations—like mushrooms—reveal their meaning only when we understand the environment in which they grow.
